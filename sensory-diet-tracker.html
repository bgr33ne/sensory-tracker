Sensory Diet Tracker: How to Track and Optimize Sensory Strategies

A sensory diet is one of the most powerful tools for supporting children with sensory processing differences. But it only works if you know what activities help, when to use them, and how effective they actually are. That's where tracking comes in.

What is a Sensory Diet?

A sensory diet is a personalized plan of activities and strategies designed to provide the sensory input a person needs to stay regulated throughout the day. Think of it like a nutritional diet—but instead of food, you're feeding the nervous system the input it craves (or carefully avoiding input that overwhelms).

The term was coined by occupational therapists Patricia Wilbarger and Julia Wilbarger in 1991. Unlike a one-time intervention, a sensory diet is an ongoing, scheduled approach to managing sensory needs proactively.

Examples of Sensory Diet Activities

  • Proprioceptive input — Heavy work like carrying groceries, push-ups against a wall, jumping on a trampoline
  • Vestibular input — Swinging, spinning, rocking, hanging upside down
  • Tactile input — Playing with playdough, fidget toys, brushing protocols
  • Auditory regulation — Noise-canceling headphones, calming music, white noise
  • Visual calming — Dimmed lights, sunglasses, reducing visual clutter
  • Oral input — Chewy snacks, drinking through a straw, crunchy foods

Why Track Your Sensory Diet?

Occupational therapists design sensory diets, but parents and caregivers implement them—in the messy reality of daily life. Tracking bridges the gap between clinical recommendations and what actually works at home.

Discover What Actually Works

Not every strategy works for every child. Weighted vests calm some children; others find them constricting. Tracking reveals which strategies consistently help in which contexts—building evidence for what belongs in your child's toolkit.

Optimize Timing

A strategy that works beautifully before school might backfire at bedtime. Tracking when you use strategies—and what follows—helps you optimize timing for maximum benefit.

Communicate with Your OT

Instead of saying "I think the brushing helps sometimes," you can show your OT data: "Brushing before transitions reduced meltdowns by 60% over three weeks." Data-driven conversations lead to better-optimized sensory diets.

Key insight: A sensory diet isn't static. Needs change with growth, seasons, stress levels, and life transitions. Ongoing tracking ensures your strategies evolve with your child.

How to Track Sensory Diet Effectiveness

Log When You Use Strategies

Note what activity you did, when you did it, and the context (before school, after meltdown, during homework, etc.).

Rate the Outcome

Did it help? A simple scale works: helped a lot, helped somewhat, no effect, made it worse. Over time, patterns emerge.

Note Conditions

The same strategy might work differently based on context. Was your child already dysregulated? Were they tired? Hungry? Capturing conditions helps you understand when to use each tool.

Review and Adjust Weekly

Spend 15 minutes each week looking at your data. Which strategies show up on good days? Which seem to have no effect? Share insights with your OT to refine the plan.

Track Strategies with Sensory Tracker

Log sensory diet activities with voice. Track effectiveness over time. See what actually works—not what you hope works.

Working with Your Occupational Therapist

OTs are trained in sensory integration theory, but they only see your child for limited sessions. Your tracking data gives them visibility into daily life they couldn't otherwise access.

Bring your strategy effectiveness data to appointments. Discuss which recommendations are working and which aren't. Collaborate on adjustments based on real evidence.
