How to Share Sensory Data with Your Therapist and Care Team

Your child's occupational therapist sees them for one hour a week. Teachers see them in a classroom of 25. The pediatrician gets a 15-minute appointment. You see them all day, every day. Sensory tracking bridges this gap—turning your observations into data that transforms how your entire care team supports your child.

The Problem: Fragmented Care

Supporting a child with sensory processing differences typically involves multiple people:

  • Parents and caregivers at home
  • Teachers and aides at school
  • Occupational therapists
  • Speech therapists, behavioral specialists
  • Pediatricians and developmental specialists

Each person sees your child in different contexts. Each has partial information. Without a shared view, strategies don't transfer between settings, patterns go unnoticed, and everyone operates on incomplete data.

Common scenario: Your OT recommends a brushing protocol. It works great at home in the morning. But you forget to tell the school, so they don't use it. Meanwhile, the teacher notices afternoon meltdowns increasing but doesn't connect it to the morning sensory routine you've stopped.

What Data to Share

With Occupational Therapists

  • Which sensory inputs trigger distress (and in what contexts)
  • Which strategies you've tried and their effectiveness
  • Patterns you've noticed across time of day, environments, or situations
  • Questions about specific sensory diet activities

With Teachers and Schools

  • Known triggers to avoid or accommodate
  • Strategies that work at home (that might transfer to school)
  • Early warning signs that indicate building overwhelm
  • What helps during and after meltdowns

With Pediatricians

  • Overall sensory profile and how it's evolving
  • Frequency and severity of sensory-related challenges
  • Impact on daily functioning (sleep, eating, social situations)
  • Response to interventions and therapies

How to Share Effectively

Summarize, Don't Dump

Nobody wants to read 30 pages of logs. Synthesize your data into key patterns: "Meltdowns dropped 50% after we started the heavy work routine before school." Lead with insights, keep raw data available for follow-up questions.

Use Consistent Language

If you describe something as "a big reaction" one day and "total meltdown" another, it's hard to compare. Consistent scales—even simple ones like mild/moderate/severe—make data more useful.

Share Proactively, Not Just Reactively

Don't wait for the crisis appointment. Regular updates help your team spot trends and adjust strategies before problems escalate.

Invite Collaboration

Sharing isn't one-way. Ask teachers what they're observing at school. Ask your OT what data would be most helpful. A shared tracking system means everyone contributes and everyone benefits.

Collaborate with Sensory Tracker

Invite your care team to view logs and insights. Everyone stays aligned with the same real-world data—no more trying to remember details at appointments.

The Benefits of Shared Sensory Data

Consistent Strategies Across Settings

When school knows what works at home (and vice versa), strategies can be applied consistently. The child experiences a more predictable world.

Faster Problem-Solving

When everyone sees the same data, discussions become more productive. Instead of "he's been having a hard time," you can discuss "sensory meltdowns increased 40% since the classroom moved to the room with fluorescent lights."

Better Treatment Decisions

OTs can refine sensory diets based on actual effectiveness data. Doctors can assess whether interventions are working. Teachers can see what accommodations actually help.

Less Repetition for Parents

Instead of explaining your child's entire history at every appointment, the data speaks for itself. More time for problem-solving, less time for catch-up.
